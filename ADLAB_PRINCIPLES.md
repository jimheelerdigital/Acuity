# ADLAB — Advertising Principles
## Universal rules for paid acquisition. Platform-agnostic. Update-proof.

---

## 1. STRUCTURE

**Consolidate, don't fragment.** One campaign per angle. One audience container per campaign. Multiple creatives inside that container. The algorithm needs concentrated data to learn — spreading budget across many small audiences starves every single one.

**Never compete against yourself.** If two campaigns target the same audience, you're bidding against your own ads and driving up your own costs. Each campaign should have a distinct angle, not a distinct audience slice.

---

## 2. OBJECTIVES

**Match the objective to your data maturity.** If you have fewer than 50 conversion events per week, optimize for traffic or clicks. The algorithm cannot learn to find converters if it's never seen one. Once you have consistent conversion volume, switch to conversion optimization.

**Start cheap, graduate to expensive.** Traffic is cheap signal. Conversions are expensive signal. Earn the right to optimize for conversions by proving your funnel works on traffic first.

---

## 3. CREATIVE

**Creative is targeting.** The algorithm uses your creative to decide who sees it. A specific ad about forgetting tasks will be shown to people who respond to that message — no interest targeting required. Generic creative gets generic audience.

**Diversity over volume.** Five creatives in different formats outperform fifteen creatives that look the same. Vary the format (walkthrough, emotional, product screenshot), the copy length (short, long, one-liner), and the headline approach (pain vs benefit).

**Show what the product does, not what it is.** Mechanism ads — step-by-step walkthroughs of the product working — educate and convert simultaneously. Someone who's never heard of you should understand the product from the ad alone.

**Refresh before fatigue.** Creative performance degrades over 3-6 weeks. Have new creative ready before the old ones die, not after. When something's winning, don't clone it — repurpose the winning message into new formats.

---

## 4. COPY

**Pain before product.** The first sentence should make someone stop scrolling because they feel called out. The product introduction comes after the emotional hook, not before.

**Specific over clever.** "Your notes app has 400 entries and zero follow-through" beats "Tired of forgetting things?" every time. Uncomfortably specific detail is what stops thumbs.

**Test copy lengths.** Some audiences read everything. Others decide in two seconds. Run short copy (2-3 sentences), long copy (5-7 sentences), and one-liners (single devastating sentence) simultaneously and let the algorithm sort it out.

---

## 5. LANDING PAGES

**The ad-to-page contract is sacred.** If the ad is about forgetting tasks, the landing page must be about forgetting tasks. Not a generic homepage. Not a broad product pitch. The emotional thread must be unbroken from ad to page to signup form.

**Every campaign gets its own landing page.** No exceptions. Generic homepage as an ad destination is the single most common reason for high click-through but zero signups.

**The landing page must explain the product.** Assume the visitor has never heard of you. They need to understand what this thing does, how it works, and what they get — within one scroll on a phone.

---

## 6. MEASUREMENT

**Clean data before optimization.** If your conversion events are firing incorrectly (duplicates, wrong pages, every page load), the algorithm will optimize toward garbage. Verify every event is accurate before spending a dollar on conversion optimization.

**Track the full funnel, not just the click.** Ad click → landing page view → signup page view → signup completion → trial activation → paid conversion. Know where the drop-off happens so you fix the right thing.

**Don't trust vanity metrics.** Impressions and clicks feel good. Signups and paid conversions are what matter. A campaign with 10,000 impressions and zero signups is worse than one with 500 impressions and two signups.

---

## 7. BUDGET

**Concentrate, don't spread.** $30/day in one place beats $10/day in three places. The algorithm needs enough daily budget to learn within a reasonable timeframe. Underfunded campaigns never exit the learning phase and waste every dollar.

**Scale gradually.** Never increase budget more than 20% at a time. Large jumps destabilize performance. Scale up over weeks, not overnight.

**Kill fast, scale slow.** Cut losers within days. Scale winners over weeks. The asymmetry is intentional — bad campaigns waste money quickly, good campaigns compound slowly.

---

## 8. TESTING

**Test one variable at a time.** If you change the image AND the copy AND the headline AND the audience, you learn nothing about what worked. Change one thing per test.

**Give tests enough time and budget to be meaningful.** A creative that's been live for 6 hours with $2 spent tells you nothing. Minimum 48 hours and enough budget to generate statistically meaningful data before judging.

**Don't edit live campaigns during the learning window.** Every significant change resets the algorithm's learning. Launch, wait, evaluate, then change. Not launch, panic, edit, panic, edit, wonder why nothing works.

---

## 9. KILL CRITERIA

**Set kill lines before you launch, not after.** Decide in advance: "If this creative spends $X with zero signups, it's dead." Emotional decisions about when to kill waste money.

**The formula:** 2x your target cost per result with zero results = kill. If you're targeting $7 per signup, kill at $15 with zero signups. No exceptions, no "let's give it one more day."

**Kill the creative, not the angle.** If 3 of 5 creatives in an experiment are dead but 2 are working, pause the losers and keep the winners running. Don't kill the whole campaign because some ads didn't work.

---

## 10. AUDIENCE

**Let the algorithm do the targeting.** Modern ad platforms are better at finding your audience than you are at defining it. Broad targeting with strong creative outperforms narrow targeting with weak creative.

**Your creative IS your targeting.** An ad about relationship stress will be shown to people experiencing relationship stress. You don't need to manually select "interested in: relationships." The content signals the audience.

**Retarget only when you have volume.** Retargeting campaigns need at least 1,000 people in the audience pool to be effective. Don't run retargeting until your top-of-funnel campaigns have built that pool.

---

## 11. BILLING

**Check billing first. Always.** When ads aren't delivering, the most common cause is a failed payment. Active campaigns with zero impressions = check billing before checking anything else. It's never the creative's fault if nothing is delivering.

---

## 12. MOBILE

**Design for phones first.** 80-90% of ad traffic is mobile. If the ad looks good on desktop but cramped on a phone, it's a bad ad. If the landing page works on desktop but requires pinching on mobile, you're losing 80% of your traffic.

**Vertical format is the default.** Most ad inventory is vertical (9:16). If you're only making square (1:1) images, you're leaving performance on the table.

---

## 13. PATIENCE

**New campaigns take time.** The first 24-48 hours of any campaign are unreliable. The algorithm is exploring, costs are high, delivery is uneven. Don't make decisions based on day-one data.

**Compounding takes weeks.** A campaign that's "fine" in week one might be excellent in week three as the algorithm learns. And a campaign that's great in week one might fatigue by week three. Judge performance over 7-14 day windows, not daily snapshots.

**The goal is learning, not immediate ROI.** Every dollar spent on a campaign that doesn't convert still teaches you something — which angles resonate, which creative formats get clicks, where the funnel breaks. That data makes the next campaign better.

---

*These principles apply regardless of which platform you're advertising on, which API parameters change, or which features Meta adds or removes. The tactics change every year. The principles don't.*
