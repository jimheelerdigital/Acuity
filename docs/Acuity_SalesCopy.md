# Acuity_SalesCopy.md

## Sales Copy Rubric — Acuity

*The rubric every piece of Acuity copy must pass before it ships. Applies to landing pages, ad scripts, onboarding, paywalls, push notifications, emails, app store listings, and anything else a prospective or paying user reads.*

| Document version | v1 · April 22, 2026 |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Purpose** | Standing reference for Claude and any human writer producing Acuity copy. Defines voice, rules, bans, and structures so output is consistent across surfaces. |
| **Companion to** | Acuity Project Brief v2 · Acuity Copy System v1 |
| **Applies to** | All customer-facing text. Internal documents, engineering comments, and admin UI are out of scope. |

---

## How to use this document

Read the whole thing before writing. Then, before shipping any piece of copy, run it against the checklist at the end. If it fails any item, rewrite until it passes. The rubric is not a suggestion — it is the minimum bar. Copy that fails the rubric is not shippable, regardless of who wrote it or how clever it sounds.

This document does not contain finished Acuity copy. For that, see the Copy System v1. This document contains only the rules the copy must follow.

# 1. The core principles

Six principles. In order of priority. When they conflict, the higher one wins.

### 1.1 Sound human, or don't ship.

Post-GPT-saturation, the default failure mode of AI-assisted copy is that it reads like AI. Sophisticated users spot it in under 4 seconds and discount the brand. The fix is not cleverness — it's specificity, proper nouns, contradictions, and rhythm breaks. AI writes smooth. Humans write broken. Acuity copy writes broken on purpose.

### 1.2 Use the customer's exact language, not yours.

Copy is not written. It is overheard. The words Maya (our ICP) types into Google, says to her therapist, and mutters to herself at 10:14 PM are the words that go in the copy. If the phrase can't be found in a Reddit thread, a therapy transcript, or a friend's text message, it doesn't belong in Acuity copy. "I can't shut my brain off" ships. "Achieve mental clarity" does not.

### 1.3 Show the artifact, not the mechanism.

Acuity's value is in the objects it produces — the weekly report, the life matrix, the memoir PDF, the pattern card. Copy should make those objects visible. Do not describe how the AI works; describe what lands on the user's phone Sunday morning. "AI-powered pattern detection" is mechanism. "Sunday morning, a 400-word story of your week" is artifact. Ship artifact.

### 1.4 Under-claim the AI.

The instinct is to foreground "AI-powered" because the product is AI-powered. Resist. Every GPT-wrapper does that, and Acuity will get lumped with them. Mention the AI only when it earns trust (in the FAQ, by naming the actual stack — Whisper and Claude), never when it substitutes for explanation.

### 1.5 Concede the weakest point first.

Maya's first objection is "another wellness app I'll use for 4 days." Copy that tries to convince her otherwise loses. Copy that admits *half the people who start stop inside a week* earns the rest of the page. Honesty is not a tone — it's a conversion mechanic. Lead with the uncomfortable truth.

### 1.6 Length is not a virtue.

If a section needs 80 words, give it 80 words. Do not pad, do not build transitions, do not write "furthermore." Cold B2C traffic under $30/mo converts on short, structural pages — median landing page in 2026 is ~4% and winners sit at 11%+, and the winners are almost always shorter than the losers. A $12.99/month app should stay under ~1,200 words on the main page.

# 2. Voice and tone

### 2.1 The Acuity voice — four characteristics

**Plainspoken.** Short sentences. Short words. If a reader needs a dictionary, rewrite. Aim for an 8th-grade reading level in acquisition copy and a 10th-grade level in longer-form surfaces (weekly reports, memoirs).

**Specific to the point of discomfort.** Name the time (10:14 PM). Name the place (bed). Name the person (your VP). Name the number (60 seconds, 400 words, $12.99). Specificity is the single biggest signal of human authorship.

**Observational, not prescriptive.** Acuity notices things. It does not command. "You had a lot on your heart tonight" beats "Take time for yourself." The app is a mirror, not a coach.

**Honest about what it isn't.** Acuity is not a therapist, not a cure, not a replacement for friends, and not a thing most people will use forever. Copy that admits these things converts better than copy that denies them.

### 2.2 The Acuity voice — what it never does

- Never yells. No exclamation points except in rare genuine-delight moments (Day 7 weekly report unlock is the main one). Zero exclamation points in ad copy.
- Never tricolons. "Fast, smart, simple" is a GPT-wrapper hallmark. Break the pattern — use two, or four, or a broken rhythm.
- Never uses em-dashes as a stylistic flourish more than once per page. One em-dash per section maximum. AI overuses them; humans don't.
- Never addresses the reader as "you" more than four times per paragraph. Switch to "most people," "some users," or a named example ("one user sent hers to her mom") to break the second-person drumbeat that reads as AI.
- Never asks rhetorical questions in the hero. "Tired of feeling overwhelmed?" is the single most AI-smelling opening in B2C wellness. Statements only above the fold.

### 2.3 Acquisition voice vs. in-product voice

Two different registers. Do not mix them.

| Surface | Primary term | Register |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Ads, landing pages, waitlist emails, App Store | **"brain dump"** | External, colloquial, slightly self-deprecating. Maya's friend's voice. |
| Onboarding, daily app copy, push notifications, weekly reports, memoirs | **"daily debrief"** | Internal, slightly more formal. The voice of a product you've chosen. |

Acquisition copy persuades. In-product copy respects. The person reading in-product copy has already paid or started a trial — they don't need to be sold, they need to be talked to like an adult.

# 3. The banned list

These words and phrases are forbidden in all Acuity customer-facing copy. Not discouraged. Banned. If any appear in a draft, rewrite.

### 3.1 Banned verbs

delve, leverage, utilize, harness, streamline, underscore, foster, empower, unlock, navigate, elevate, optimize, maximize, embark, embrace, revolutionize, transform (unless quoting a user), curate, craft (unless literal), supercharge, level up, dive deep, dive in

### 3.2 Banned adjectives

pivotal, robust, innovative, seamless, cutting-edge, game-changing, best-in-class, holistic, mindful (unless quoting a user), intentional, meaningful (when used as filler), thoughtful (when used as filler), powerful (unless followed by a specific), comprehensive

### 3.3 Banned nouns

landscape, realm, tapestry, synergy, testament, underpinnings, journey, ecosystem, solution (for "our product"), offering, space (as in "the mental health space"), tool (prefer "app"), platform (prefer "app"), experience (as a synonym for "product")

### 3.4 Banned transitions

furthermore, moreover, consequently, notably, importantly, in today's fast-paced world, whether you're…, at its core, more than just, it's more than, simply put, in essence

### 3.5 Banned structures

- Three-adjective tricolons, especially with alliteration ("simple, smart, seamless")
- Rhetorical questions as headlines ("Tired of…?" "Ever feel like…?" "What if…?")
- "Imagine if…" openings
- "The future of X is Y" framings
- "We believe" / "We think" / "Our mission" in acquisition copy (belongs on About, not hero)
- "Say goodbye to…" / "Say hello to…" openers
- Hero headlines that are slogans ("Think better. Feel better. Live better.")

### 3.6 Banned claims

- "AI-powered" above the fold
- "Science-backed" unless we can cite a specific study in the same section
- "Therapist-approved" unless a named therapist approved it
- Round-number social proof without a name ("10,000+ users," "join thousands")
- "Loved by…" followed by a list of publications we haven't been featured in

### 3.7 Conditional bans

"Journal" / "journaling" as a category word is banned in all acquisition copy. Acuity's category is the shutdown ritual, not journaling. Rosebud owns "journaling" and has $6M to defend it. The word may appear inside quoted user testimonials if a user genuinely said it, and in SEO meta descriptions where search intent demands it, but never in the first viewport of the homepage, never in ad headlines, and never in the first line of any landing page angle.

"AI" is discouraged but not banned. Use only when it earns trust — in the FAQ, when naming the actual stack (Whisper and Claude), or when the user's objection is literally "is this just ChatGPT with a microphone?" Never in the hero. Never as a selling point.

# 4. Harry Dry's three rules

Every sentence of Acuity copy must pass all three tests. If a sentence fails any test, rewrite.

### 4.1 Specific — can you picture it?

"Talk for 60 seconds" passes. "Save time on reflection" fails.
"Sunday morning, read the story of your week" passes. "Get meaningful insights" fails.
"Your VP's name gets mentioned three times this week" passes. "Patterns emerge" fails.

The test: if a designer could sketch the sentence as an illustration, it passes. If not, rewrite.

### 4.2 Falsifiable — could it be wrong?

"14 days free. No card." passes — it's either true or not.
"Change your relationship with your thoughts" fails — it's untestable.
"A 400-word narrative of your week" passes — it's either 400 words or not.
"Deeper self-understanding" fails — there is no number that proves or disproves it.

The test: if two people could disagree about whether the claim is true, rewrite until they can't.

### 4.3 Unique — could a competitor say it?

If Rosebud, Stoic, Day One, Mindsera, Reflection, or any of the 400 GPT-wrapper journaling apps could put the same sentence on their homepage without changing a word, Acuity cannot use it. "Track your mood" is not unique. "Every Sunday morning, a 400-word story of your week" is unique because no competitor ships that artifact.

The test: would a competitor's copywriter steal this line? If yes, write a line they can't steal.

# 5. Schwartz awareness levels

Every piece of acquisition copy must be written for a specific awareness level. Never mix levels on the same surface — the copy will feel unfocused and convert nobody.

The five levels, mapped to Acuity:

### 5.1 Unaware

Doesn't know they have the problem. The ad names the problem while they're doing it. Product mention comes late (0:12+ in a 25-second ad).
*Example anchor:* "It's 10 PM. You're in bed. Your brain is running through every conversation you had today."

### 5.2 Problem-aware

Knows the problem, doesn't know solutions exist. The copy names a search they've run or a phrase they've typed.
*Example anchor:* "If you've ever Googled 'how to stop overthinking at night,' this is for you."

### 5.3 Solution-aware

Knows solutions exist (journaling, therapy, meditation), hasn't committed to one for this app's specific reason.
*Example anchor:* "This is the fifth journal I've stopped using. So I built one I actually use."

### 5.4 Product-aware

Has tried or considered competitors. Copy respects that history by naming competitors directly.
*Example anchor:* "I tried Rosebud, Reflection, Day One, Stoic. Here's what bugged me."

### 5.5 Most aware

Waitlist users, retargeting, lapsed trial users. Copy removes friction, does not persuade.
*Example anchor:* "Quick update for the waitlist. We're live."

Rule of thumb: **cold Meta traffic is 70% problem-aware, 20% unaware, 10% solution-aware.** Copy the landing page for problem-aware traffic and run specific ads for the other two segments.

# 6. Structural patterns that convert

The patterns, ordered by where they appear.

### 6.1 The hero

One line that names the user's state. One line that names the mechanism. One CTA that removes friction.

The hero has three jobs and only three: (a) make the visitor feel seen in 2 seconds, (b) explain what Acuity does in 1 sentence, (c) remove the two biggest barriers to clicking (cost and card).

*Structure:*
> [VOC problem statement]
> [Mechanism in one falsifiable sentence]
> [Speak whatever's on your mind before bed. Acuity turns it into tasks, tracks what's weighing on you, and every Sunday morning writes you a 400-word story of your week.]
> [CTA button]
> [Friction-removal caption: "No card. 90 seconds to set up."]

Never pricing in the hero. Never "AI-powered" in the hero. Never a rhetorical question in the hero. Never a three-word tagline in the hero.

### 6.2 Proof strip

Social proof in the second viewport. Named person + specific quote + date beats round-number count every time in 2026.

*Structure:*
> [Star rating] · [Publication feature, if real] · **"[Specific quote that could only come from a real user]"** — [First name + last initial, date]

Do not say "10,000+ users." Do not say "join thousands." Use one named person with one specific sentence.

### 6.3 How it works

Three steps. Each step is one line. Each line passes Harry Dry's three rules.

*Structure:*
> **1. [Verb].** [Specific action, specific time.]
> **2. [Verb].** [Specific output, specific when.]
> **3. [Verb].** [The artifact that sells the product.]

### 6.4 Features as artifacts

Do not list features. List objects. Each feature gets a name, a one-sentence description of what it does, and a specific detail that makes it picturable.

*Structure:*
> **[Proper-noun name of the feature]**
> [One sentence of what it is, in plain language.]
> [One specific detail — a number, a quote, a real example.]

Bad: "Advanced pattern detection powered by AI."
Good: "Patterns You Didn't Clock. 'You feel most energized on days you mention exercise — mood averages 7.2 vs. 4.8.' Acuity finds these and shows them."

### 6.5 Objection handlers

List the objections in the order the customer raises them, not the order that feels best to us. Concede the weakest one first. Each answer is 1–3 sentences. Never defensive.

*Structure:*
> **"[Exact verbatim objection in quotes]"**
> [Answer that either concedes, reframes, or provides specific evidence. Never defensive. Never salesy.]

### 6.6 Pricing

Pricing never goes in the hero for a category-creation product. It goes after the explainer and after the objections. The pricing section has four components: the price, the trial terms, the cancellation terms, and a data-retention promise.

*Structure:*
> **Free for 14 days. No card. Then $[price]/month.**
> [Cancellation language.] [Data retention language.]
> [CTA button]

### 6.7 FAQ

Six questions maximum. Not a dumping ground. Each question is a real objection phrased as the user would phrase it. Name the actual tech stack (Whisper, Claude) in the FAQ — it builds trust because GPT-wrappers don't do this.

### 6.8 Final CTA

The only emotional line on the page. Earns the right to be emotional because it comes after the visitor has been taught what the product is. Re-anchors to the trigger moment in the hero.

*Structure:*
> [Emotional re-anchor to the 10 PM moment or equivalent trigger.]
> [CTA button]
> [Friction-removal caption]

# 7. Positioning rules specific to Acuity

Non-negotiable framing decisions. Do not override without a founder sign-off.

### 7.1 Category

Acuity is a **shutdown ritual**. Not a journal. Not a mood tracker. Not a wellness app. Not a productivity tool. The shutdown ritual category is borrowed from Cal Newport's *Deep Work*, which the target audience already knows. When writing copy, ask: does this sentence reinforce the shutdown-ritual framing, or does it drift toward journaling?

### 7.2 The hero conversion driver is the weekly report, not the daily recording.

Users sign up because of the promise of the Sunday report, even though the daily recording is what they'll actually do. All acquisition copy should foreground the weekly report as the artifact. The daily brain dump is the input; the weekly report is the output worth paying for.

### 7.3 Memory is the product. Not intelligence.

The differentiator against GPT-wrappers is not that Acuity is smart. It's that Acuity remembers. When the user says "call the accountant" in week one and again in week four, Acuity notices. That's the sell. "Memory" and "remembers" are approved words. "Intelligence" and "insights" (as standalone benefit) are discouraged.

### 7.4 The founder is on camera, not in the copy.

Keenan appears in ads (real camera, not avatar). Keenan signs emails. Keenan does not appear in the landing page as a founder letter. Founder vulnerability belongs in ads and emails, not on the homepage.

### 7.5 Never promise therapeutic outcomes.

Acuity is not a medical device, not a mental health intervention, and not a therapist. Copy can describe what the app does (transcribes, extracts, summarizes, writes a report). Copy cannot claim it will reduce anxiety, cure insomnia, improve mental health outcomes, or replace therapy. "What happens the other 167 hours of the week" is the approved framing around therapy.

### 7.6 Privacy is a structural claim, not a tone.

Every privacy statement must be specific and verifiable. "Your data is secure" fails. "Voice recordings are deleted within 24 hours of transcription" passes. "Nobody at Acuity can read your transcript — the database is encrypted and my read access is gated behind a 2FA key I don't have easy access to" passes at a higher level because it names the mechanism.

When describing the AI stack in privacy contexts, the accurate framing is: *"Under OpenAI's and Anthropic's API terms, what we send them is processed and returned — it is not used to train their models."* Do not say "both refuse to train on user data" — that's imprecise. The correct framing is that API customers are not opted into training by default, which is different from consumer ChatGPT.

# 8. Support mode mapping

Acuity ships four in-product support modes (Accountability, Clarity, Emotional, Productivity). Copy tone must match mode for in-product surfaces. For acquisition surfaces, use the mapping below.

| Surface | Default support mode | Rationale |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Landing page (homepage) | **Clarity** leaning **Emotional** | Cold traffic needs to feel seen first, understood second. Analytical tone with warm phrasing. |
| /for/founders and /for/decoded | **Clarity** leaning **Productivity** | Self-optimizers respond to analytical, efficient framing. |
| /for/therapy and /for/sleep | **Emotional** leaning **Clarity** | Therapy-adjacent traffic needs warmth before analysis. |
| Meta ads (cold) | **Clarity** | Keenan-on-camera is observational, not exhortative. |
| Onboarding | **Clarity** until Screen 6, then user-selected | Screen 6 hands control to the user. |
| Day-14 paywall | **Accountability** | Direct, matter-of-fact, "keep what you've built." Not warm. |
| Push notifications | **User-selected mode** | The tone the user chose during onboarding. Never override. |
| Founder emails | **Clarity** with a trace of self-deprecation | Keenan's voice. Honest, plain, signed. |

Productivity mode should rarely appear outside the in-product experience. It reads as cold on acquisition surfaces and lowers conversion among Emotional and Clarity users, who are the majority of our ICP.

# 9. The pre-ship checklist

Before any piece of copy ships, run this list. If any item fails, rewrite until it passes.

### 9.1 Voice check
- [ ] Zero banned verbs, adjectives, nouns, or transitions (§3)
- [ ] No three-adjective tricolons
- [ ] No rhetorical questions in the hero or in the first line of any section
- [ ] No more than one em-dash per section
- [ ] No more than four "you"s per paragraph without a break
- [ ] Zero exclamation points in acquisition copy (one allowed in Day 7 weekly-report push)

### 9.2 Specificity check
- [ ] Every sentence passes Harry Dry's three rules: specific, falsifiable, unique (§4)
- [ ] At least one proper noun, number, or named example per 100 words
- [ ] No claim without a falsifiable detail backing it

### 9.3 Positioning check
- [ ] The word "journaling" does not appear in acquisition copy (§7.1)
- [ ] "AI-powered" does not appear above the fold (§3.6)
- [ ] The weekly report is named or visible within the first viewport (§7.2)
- [ ] "Memory" or "remembers" appears at least once if the copy is longer than 300 words (§7.3)
- [ ] No therapeutic outcome claims (§7.5)
- [ ] Privacy claims are specific and mechanism-level, not vibes-level (§7.6)

### 9.4 Structural check
- [ ] Hero has: VOC problem, mechanism sub, CTA, friction-removal caption
- [ ] Pricing is below the fold, never in the hero (§6.6)
- [ ] Social proof uses named person + quote + date, not round numbers (§6.2)
- [ ] Objections are listed in the customer's raising order, weakest-first (§6.5)
- [ ] Final CTA re-anchors to the trigger moment (§6.8)

### 9.5 Awareness check
- [ ] The piece is written for a single Schwartz level, not a mix (§5)
- [ ] The hook matches the level (unaware names the problem; most-aware removes friction)

### 9.6 Support mode check
- [ ] The tone matches the surface per the table in §8
- [ ] In-product copy reflects the user's selected mode when the mode is set
- [ ] Paywall copy is Accountability tone (direct, "keep" framing)

### 9.7 The single hardest test
Read the copy out loud at 10:14 PM in a dark room, as the last thing before sleep. If any sentence feels like it was written by a committee, a marketer, or an AI — rewrite it. Maya is going to read this with a glowing phone an inch from her face. She has read ten thousand landing pages. She recognizes slop in under 4 seconds. The copy that survives that test is the copy that converts.

## Closing note

This rubric is not a creativity ceiling. It is a floor. Copy that clears the floor can be as surprising, specific, and funny as it wants to be — in fact, that's what the floor is designed to allow. The bans exist so that energy goes into distinctive lines instead of boilerplate.

When in doubt, the hierarchy is: **sound human > follow the rubric > follow the brand guide > follow convention.** The rubric is here because it protects the voice, not because the voice is defined by rules.

Update this document when we learn something new from the first 100 trial users. Until then, treat it as the operating definition of Acuity copy.
